NOTE — METHODOLOGY

About this project

What the model does, and where it falls short

A short account of the formula, the data it assumes, and the cases where it should not be trusted.

§1 — FORMULA

The HDI formula

The Human Development Index combines three sub-indices, health, education, and income, into a single score between 0 and 1, using a geometric mean rather than a simple average. That choice penalizes countries that are lopsided: very high income with poor health, for example, does not buy back a high score the way an arithmetic average would allow.

Each sub-index is built by rescaling a raw indicator against fixed minimum and maximum goalposts, the same approach the UNDP has used since the index's 2010 methodology revision. This demo reproduces that formula directly in the browser rather than calling a hosted model, so the numbers it returns will track the official UNDP statistics closely for any country whose raw indicators are entered accurately.

§2 — INPUTS

Indicators and goalposts

The model takes four raw figures and produces three sub-indices:

Life expectancy at birthGoalposts: 20 – 85 years
Mean & expected years of schoolingGoalposts: 0 – 18 years
GNI per capita, PPPGoalposts: $100 – $75,000, log-scaled

Income is log-scaled before rescaling, reflecting the diminishing returns of additional income on wellbeing at higher levels, consistent with the UNDP's published technical notes.

§3 — TIERS

Development tiers

The demo reports a tier alongside the raw score, following the UNDP's standard breakpoints:

Very High≥ 0.800
High0.700 – 0.799
Medium0.550 – 0.699
Low< 0.550
§4 — LIMITS

Limitations

The HDI is a composite of three dimensions only; it omits inequality, environmental sustainability, gender gaps, and political freedom, each of which the UNDP tracks separately through complementary indices. A score from this tool describes average national attainment, not distribution across a population, and should not stand in for those other measures.

This page runs the published formula directly rather than a fitted statistical model, so its accuracy depends entirely on the accuracy of the four numbers you provide, not on any training data of its own.
